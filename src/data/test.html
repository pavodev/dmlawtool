The Choice:


You are in this knot because you’re doing a research and you don’t fall under the exception that requires that the identity of the subject is not relevant (and therefore, you wouldn’t need the subject’s consent). Thus, now, you need the subject’s consent in order to lawfully collect and process their personal data.
Before choosing whether you can anonymize data or not, it is important to clearly understand how to ask a consent, when a consent is valid, and what to do in particular cases (retroactive consent). 


The most common solution is to obtain the data subject’s valid consent, but the law provides other exception. In the field of research, an important exception is the processing of personal data not related to a specific person for the purpose of research or statistics.

Consent

A consent is a permission for something to happen or agreement to do something (Oxford Languages Dictionary). In a legal language, a consent is a particular type of legitimating motive. This motive let the other person do something that they shouldn’t do without it.  The data processor must ask for the consent directly to the data subject, who must be capable of discernment in order to lawfully give their consent. If the subject doesn’t have a full capacity of discernment (which has to be specifically evaluated from case to case), the consent must be sought from the legal representative (the parents or the guardian). Consent is not needed for a deceased person.  A particular form of the consent is not foreseen by the law.

However, a correct request for a consent must contain the following information: 

  • the specific purpose of the data collection
  • how the personal data will be processed, what will be done with the personal data
  • if the personal data will be pseudonymized or anonymized and when
  • how long are the personal data be kept for
  • if the personal data will be given to third parties
  • the processor’s contact, because the subject has the right to contact the processor anytime to ask anything concerning their personal data processing
The subject has the right to: 
  • refuse the processing of their personal data
  • anytime withdraw their consent until the moment data are anonymous. That means that if the subject requires their personal data to be erased, the processor must do so.
  • Take an informed decision concerning giving or not giving their consent
The consent of the subject must be: 
  • specific to the purpose asked for
  • freely given: the subject must not endure negative consequences in case of refusal
  • clear, unambiguous
  • given by a clear affirmative action, whether the action is verbal or written. The consent may also be implicit given the circumstances, but it is better when it is clearly written.
 

Example of explicit consent:
both parts sigh a contract where there is a consent clause.

Examples of implicit consent based on the circumstances:
the subject accepts to answer several questions concerning his opinion made by a survey institute, after he’s being clearly informed of the survey’s purpose and that all results will be published. His consent is valid, because his implicit consent is based on the circumstances that he freely answered the questions and he was aware of where his personal data would be published.

Be aware of / Pay attention to:


You are responsible for the way you process and archive data also when you process data that were collected by somebody else. Thus, even when dealing with already existing data, it is important to check if the data had been collected lawfully and with a valid consent or if you need for example a retroactive or a new consent.
